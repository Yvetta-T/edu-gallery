My Mother Li Yuehua: Building Careers Through Judo

My mother, Li Yuehua, didn’t just teach blind athletes to throw opponents—she taught them to throw society’s limitations.

For 21 years, her judo program has been a bridge to dignity:

17 national champions now work as coaches, referees, and community leaders, proving disability is no barrier to purpose.

When government support was absent, she mortgaged our home to build a training center.

Athletes like Liu Li and Shi Yijie—once labeled "helpless"—became world champions and role models.

Mom’s philosophy? "A throw on the mat is a step toward independence." She designed career pathways: medalists mentor new recruits, while others join her in advocating for inclusive policies.

I chose psychology at UC Berkeley to help athletes cope with pressure, committing to special education programs now.

Her athletes’ trophies are not just metal—they’re passports. One gold medalist opened a massage therapy clinic employing blind graduates; another teaches self-defense to visually impaired teens.

Mom’s greatest victory? Proving blindness isn’t the end of a story—it’s the first throw in a lifelong match.

As she says, "We don’t train bodies—we train futures." Every grip, every fall, every gold is a declaration: disability fuels greatness.

Liu Li: The Unseen Force of Destiny

Born in 1998 on the rural outskirts of Changsha, Liu Li’s life began under the weight of genetic fragility.

She and her sister were born with unusually narrow eyes and diagnosed with Marfan Syndrome—a connective tissue disorder that can cause life-threatening aortic rupture.

Orphaned at the age of nine, Liu Li lost both parents and was raised by her 60-year-old grandmother.

In 2008, at a time when China’s special education system often segregated visually impaired children in schools like hidden cattle, ten-year-old Liu Li enrolled in the blind division of Changsha Special Education School.

A decade later, in 2019, doctors discovered her lens had dislocated. One hard fall, they warned, could rupture her eyeball. She would need to give up judo and rest.

"I cried, not because it hurt," she recalls, "but because I thought losing my sight meant I had nothing left—until even that was gone."

But Coach Li Yuehua became her anchor, whispering the words she never forgot: “I’m your eyes.”

At the 2024 Paralympics in Paris, Liu faced Brazilian veteran de Freitas in the final. Despite a severe ankle injury during the match, she fought through the pain and won.

Post-match scans revealed a complete ligament rupture in her right ankle—an injury that required six months of rehabilitation.

Today, Liu trains with a customized ankle brace, carefully avoiding impact that could detach her retinas.

“They say my eyes make me vulnerable,” she says, “but vulnerability is just strength, waiting to be folded into a new shape.”

Her victory isn’t measured by medals—but by how loudly sighted and blind children laugh together when they fall in the classroom. Because to Liu Li, that’s the real triumph.

Wang Yue: The Higher Education Barrier for China's Blind Athletes

Wang isn’t not only just a Games Judo 2nd place winner—she’s also my closest friend. At 28, she should be weighing offers from top universities.

Instead, she’s caught in a system that celebrates her victories on the mat while denying her access to the classroom.

Her dream of attending Tsinghua University was dismissed with a single line: “No braille-compatible curriculum.”

This isn't just Wang’s struggle—it reflects a national failure. Top Chinese universities accept athletes with physical disabilities, but blind students remain locked out.

Screen readers can’t interpret math symbols or music notation. Only a fraction of textbooks exist in braille.

For blind athletes like Wang, whose training runs from dawn until night, traditional education simply doesn’t fit their reality.

Most are funneled into state-assigned massage jobs. It’s not a lack of ambition that limits them—it’s a lack of access.

Wang hasn’t given up. And neither have I.

As a future educator and advocate, I don’t see Wang as the exception—I see her as a mirror of all the potential that remains untapped because of systemic neglect.

“They call us inspirations,” Wang once told me. “But what I want is education, not applause.”

She deserves both.

Shi Yijie: Disability, Education, and Athletic Triumph in China

Shi Yijie, born in 2000 in Hunan, lost her sight at age 12 due to retinitis pigmentosa.

Her life reflects the systemic barriers disabled individuals face in China’s education system—and how state-sponsored sports often become their only path forward.

At 13, she joined para judo. “Sports became my education,” she says—a sentiment many disabled youth echo.

She later trained at the Chaohui Sports Training Centre, with coaches using tactile techniques to accommodate her blindness.

Shi rose to gold medals at IBSA and Para Olympic Games, but her story also reveals that only 0.5% of China’s disabled population accesses inclusive education.

From a segregated classroom to the global stage, her journey challenges how China defines disability.

“I want to prove blindness isn’t the end,” she says. Her fight in Paris is not just for gold—but for dignity, inclusion, and the right to be seen.

Zhu Shiwen: Love, Resilience, and Rethinking Disability in China

Zhu Shiwen, born in 2001 and diagnosed with Leber’s Hereditary Optic Neuropathy, is a Paralympic judo star and Shi Yijie’s partner.

Their families disapprove, but together they’re redefining what it means to live with blindness—and love fully.

Zhu, passionate about English, often uses Siri to talk to foreign athletes—but without access to proper instruction, conversations end with frustration.

His story reflects how education, especially language, remains inaccessible to disabled athletes.

Inclusion isn’t about logistics—it’s about trust and autonomy. Zhu shows us that with the right support, disabled individuals lead—not follow.

Just 13 days before the 2024 Turkey Grand Prix, Zhu dislocated his elbow—yet still medaled in J1 -60kg and qualified for Paris.

Zhu and Shi aren’t just athletes—they’re advocates, partners, and pioneers of a more inclusive future.
